IRAN CYBERWARFARE DOCTRINE

Iran's cyberwarfare doctrine constitutes the following:

  1. Asymmetric Warfare - Iran views cyberattacks as help to confront more powerful adversaries.
  2. Organizational Structure - Iran has created a sophisticated organizational structure to manage cyber conflict, including:

      3. The Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC)

      The Basij, a civilian paramilitary organization

      The Passive Defense Organization (NPDO)

      The Supreme Council of Cyberspace for coordination

  3. Defensive and Offensive Capabilities - Iran focuses on both protecting its own infrastructure and developing offensive cyber capabilities.
  4. Use of Proxies - Iran often leverages third parties and proxy groups to carry out cyberattacks, making attribution more difficult.
  5. Retaliation and Deterrence - Iran sees cyberattacks as part of a continuum of conflict and uses them for retaliation against perceived threats.
  6. Intelligence Gathering - Iran conducts cyber espionage operations to collect intelligence on adversaries and dissidents.
  7. Domestic Control - Cyberwarfare capabilities are used internally for surveillance and control of Iran's own population.
  8. Proportionality - Iranian cyber retaliation tends to be proportionate in scale to the perceived offense.
  9. Integration with Broader Strategy - Cyberwarfare is viewed as part of Iran's broader "soft war" military strategy.

The following are Iran's key cyber laws and regulations:

  1. The Computer Crimes Law of 2010
    • SUMMARY: This legislation entitles a series of laws that cover the following crimes: those against data privacy and computer systems, those against data integrity and authenticity, computer-related theft and fraud, and those against public morality.
    • KEY ARTICLES:
      • Article 1: Unauthorized access to secured government data and systems is criminalized.
      • Article 2: Unauthorized access to non-public communications is criminalized.
      • Article 3: Access to and sharing of confidential government information is criminalized.
      • Article 5: Trained officials responsible for protecting confidential data are criminally liable for Article 3 offenses.
      • Article 14: Engaging with obscene content via digital systems is criminalized.
      • Article 16: Altering and misusing someone's digital media to harm their reputation is criminalized.
      • Article 17: Publishing someone's private media or secrets without consent to damage their reputation is criminalized.
      • Article 18: Disseminating lies to harm the public or authorities, or falsely associating individuals with lies, is criminalized.
    • PENALTIES:
      • Article 1: 91 days to 1 year in prison and/or a fine of 5 million - 20 million Rials.
      • Article 2: Prison sentence and fines (not specifically known).
      • Article 3: Fines, 2-10 years or 5-15 years in prison.
      • Article 5: Severe criminal penalties.
      • Article 14: 91 days - 2 years in prison and/or a fine of 5 million - 40 million Rials, or death sentence if found to be “mofsed-e-filarz".
      • Article 16: 2 years in prison and a fine of 40 million Rials.
      • Article 17: 91 days - 2 years in prison and/or a fine of 5 million - 40 million Rials.
      • Article 18: 91 days - 2 years in prison and/or a fine of 5 million - 40 million Rials.
  2. The Electronic Commerce Law of the Islamic Republic of Iran (2004):
    • SUMMARY: This legislation addresses e-commerce transactions and data protection. It covers obligations for parties in online contracts, consumer rights regarding e-commerce, and protection of personal data.
    • KEY ARTICLES:
      • Articles 33-37: Outlines the regulations sellers and service providers must follow in electronic transactions.
      • Article 51: Suppliers advertising harmful products face prosecution.
      • Article 58: Handling sensitive personal data without explicit consent is illegal.
      • Article 66: Misleading use of trademarks in online transactions is illegal.
      • Article 67: Deceiving others or systems in electronic transactions for financial gain is illegal.
      • Article 68: Falsifying data messages or signatures through unauthorized methods is illegal.
      • Article 74: Violating copyright laws in electronic transactions is prosecutable.
      • Article 75: Illegally obtaining or disclosing trade secrets through electronic means is prohibited.
    • PENALTIES:
      • Articles 33-37: Fine of 10 million - 50 million Rials.
      • Article 51: 1-3 years in prison and a fine of 100 million Rials.
      • Article 58: 1-3 years in prison.
      • Article 66: 1-3 years in prison and a fine of 20 million - 100 million Rials.
      • Article 67: 1-3 years in prison and a fine equal to the illegal financial gain.

General Data Protection Regulation - (GDPR)

  • What is GDPR?
    • Data protection law that applies to organizations handling personal data for EU residents.
  • Core Principles:
    1. Lawfulness, Fairness, and Transparency
      • In data processing.
    2. Purpose Limitation
      • Collect data for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes.
    3. Data Minimization
      • Limit data collection to what is necessary.
    4. Accuracy
      • Limit data collection to what is necessary.
    5. Storage Limitations
      • Keep data only as long as necessary.
    6. Integrity and Confidentiality
      • Ensure appropriate security measures.
    7. Accountability
      • Remain accountable and ensure data protection.
  • Consent:
    • Organizations must obtain clear, affirmative consent for data processing.
    • Organizations must honor data subject rights, including access, rectification, erasure, and data portability.
  • Data Security:
    • Implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to protect data.
    • Conduct Data Protection Impact Assessments.
    • Maintain records of processing activities.
  • Breach Notifications:
    • Report data breaches to supervisory authorities within 72 hours.
    • Notify affected individuals of high-risk breaches.
  • Compliance Steps:
    1. Conduct a data audit.
    2. Establish a lawful basis for data processing.
    3. Update privacy notices and consent mechanisms.
    4. Implement data subject request procedures.
    5. Enhance data security measures.
    6. Train staff on GDPR requirements.
    7. Review and update contracts with data processors.
    8. Develop a breach response plan.
  • Penalties:
    • The severity of the penalty relies on 10 criteria:
      • Gravity and nature
      • Intention
      • Mitigation
      • Precautionary measures
      • History
      • Cooperation
      • Cooperation
      • Data category
      • Proactive reporting
      • Certification
      • Aggravating/mitigating factors
    • Depending on the severity of the infringement, two different fines can be handed out:
      • In a lower severity case, an organization can be fined up to €10 million or 2% of the firm’s worldwide annual revenue from the previous financial year, whichever is higher.
      • In a higher severity case, up to €20 million or 4% of the firm’s worldwide annual revenue from the previous financial year, whichever is higher.
    • There are other punishments besides just fines:
      • Warnings.
      • Temporary or permanent bans on data processing.
      • Orders to rectify, restrict, or erase data.
      • Suspension of data transfers.
  • Top Fines:
    • Meta: €1.2 billion ($1.3 billion) in 2023 for violating data transfer regulations.
    • Amazon: €746 million ($781 million) in 2021 for non-compliant advertising practices.
    • Instagram: €405 million ($427 million) in 2022 for violating children’s online privacy.

(Vora, 2024)


Network and Information Security Directive 2 - (EU)

  • Timeline:
    • Updated version of the Network and Information Security directive from 2016.
    • Recently enacted in January of 2023.
    • Member states must transpose NIS2 into national law by October 17, 2024.
    • Organizations must comply with the requirements by October 18, 2024.
  • Purpose:
    • NIS2 aims to establish a high common level of cybersecurity across the EU by:
      • Expanding the number of covered sectors from 7 to 15.
        • Previously Covered Sectors:
        • Energy
        • Banking
        • Drinking Water
        • Digital Infrastructure
        • Digital providers
        • Health
        • Transport
        • Newly Covered Sectors:
        • Research
        • Space
        • Public Administration
        • Waste and Wastewater Management
        • Postal and Courier Services
        • ICT Service Management
        • Providers of publicly available electronic communication services
        • Production & processing (food, medical, products, chemicals, and more)
      • Dramatically increasing the requirements for enforcing cybersecurity. Non-compliance can lead to legal ramifications for management teams.
  • Requirements:
    • Organizations covered by NIS2 must implement:
      • Risk assessment and management procedures.
      • Incident detection and response capabilities.
      • Regular cybersecurity audits.
      • Supply chain security measures.
      • Encryption and vulnerability disclosure processes.
  • Reporting:
    • Authorities must be notified of significant cybersecurity incidents within 24 hours.
    • Organizations must provide updates on mitigation measures taken.
  • Enforcement and Penalties:
    • Fines of up to €10 million or 2% of global annual turnover for non-compliance.
    • Potential personal liability for management teams.

(LAMCOM, 2024)


Italy Cybercrime Laws:

  • Article 615 ter of the Italian Criminal Code:
    • Provides punishment for unauthorized access to computers and IT systems.
      • Can be sentenced to up to three years in prison.
    • If a person uses their access for different reasons than those of which they were authorized, it is considered a criminal offense.
    • It has also been decided that a password-protected Dropbox account can be considered an IT system under this law.
  • Article 635 Quater:
    • Punishes Denial-of-Service attacks.
    • “Those who destroy, damage, or make unusable by ‘introducing or transmitting’ data, information, or programs, or severely impair their working of other people’s computers or IT systems” (Bianchi & Bevilacqua, 2023).
      • Violators are subject to imprisonment for 1 to 5 years.
  • Article 640 ter:
    • Punishes IT fraud, such as phishing.
    • Stealing or using a person's digital identity to carry out IT fraud.
      • Can result in imprisonment for 2 to 6 years and a fine from €600 to €3,000.
  • Article 615 quinquies:
    • Punishes those who use malware to infect IT systems.
      • Subject to up to 2 years in prison.
      • Fines not exceeding €10,329.

Federal Cyber Laws:


  • Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA):
    • Enacted in 1986
    • Amends Comprehensive Crime Control Act of 1984
      • First major cybercrime legislation and was not very effective
    • Prohibits intentionally accessing a computer without authorization or exceeding authorization
    • Has been amended 9 times between 1988 and 2008
    • Amendments Include:
      • Criminalizing distribution of malicious code
      • Criminalizing denial of service attacks
      • Criminalizing accessing, tampering, and attempted extortion
      • Criminalizing trafficking in passwords or other access control mechanisms
      • Expanding protection to private computers
      • Including punishing taking information off of systems
      • Allowing civil action to be taken
        • Now companies can threaten use of this act to prevent others from leaking private information
        • But can also get judgement easier when evidence isn’t rock solid because proof doesn’t need to be “beyond a reasonable doubt”

    • Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act (CISA):

      • Passed in 2015
      • Key Terms:
        • Cyber Threat Indicators: necessary information to identify threats and information on actual or potential harm as a result of a cyber threat
        • Defense Measures: something that detects, prevents, or mitigates a known or suspected cyber threat or security vulnerability
        • Cybersecurity Purpose: purpose of protecting an information system or information from a cyber threat or security vulnerability
      • Encourages private companies to voluntarily share information by allowing federal antitrust exemptions, immunity from federal/state disclosure laws, and a non-waiver of applicable protections for sharing materials
      • Before, companies didn't share cyber threats for fear of violating regulations so this act offers protections so companies will disclose vulnerabilities to improve overall cybersecurity
      • Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) becomes the central hub for sharing of defensive measures and cyber threat indicators between private and public sectors

    State Cyber Laws:

    • Security Breach Notification Laws
    • Summary: Private organizations or Government Institutions operating in the US, are required by law to send out alerts when a data breach occurs, leaking personally identifiable information about individuals.
    • State-Level governments may legislate these practices differently from state to state.
    • Wyoming: Wyo. Stat Wyo. Stat
    • Only applies to private organizations. Data theft from Government organizations may be handled differently.
    • Criminalizes:
      • Failure to notify affected individual when security breach occurs. (Wyo. Stat §§ 40-12-502f)
      • Failure to notify the Attorney General if data breach affects more than 10000 residents. (Wyo. Stat §§ 40-12-502d)
      • Failure to provide reasonable security measures when handling sensitive user data.(§§ 40-12-504e)
      • Failure to dispose sensitive legacy, or unauthorized data properly.(Wyo. Stat §§ 40-12-503e-f)
      • Providing false information to regulators. (Wyo. Stat §§ 6-5-308)
    • Offenses (Wyo. Stat §§ 40-12-206):
      • Fines may vary based on the severity of violation. (Wyo. Stat §§ 40-12-508b)
      • Affected individuals may be given the right to pursue legal action against the breached organization. (Wyo. Stat §§ 40-12-108b)
      • Misdemeanor.
      • Unintentional violation: (Wyo. Stat §§ 6-5-308)
        • Up to a $750 fine per violation.
        • Possible 6 months imprisonment.
      • Intentional Violation: (Wyo. Stat §§ 6-5-308)
        • Up to a $10,000 per violation.
        • Possible 1 year imprisonment.
    • Massachusetts: Mass. Gen Laws. 
      • Applies to both Private and Government organizations
      • Criminalizes
        • Failure to notify affected individuals when a data breach occurs. (Mass. Gen Laws § 93H-3b)
        • Failure to implement reasonable security measures.(Mass. Gen Laws § 93H-2a)
        • Failure to notify Massachusetts Attorney General. (Mass. Gen Laws § 93H-3b)
        • Providing false information to regulators. (Mass. Gen Laws § 93H-5)
      • Offenses
        • Enforcement from the FTC. (Mass. Gen Laws § 93A-2x)
        • Criminal offense. (Mass. Gen Laws § 93A-4)
        • $5000 fine per violation. (Mass Gen Laws § 93A-4)
        • Affected individuals may be granted the right to pursue legal action against the breached organization. (Mass. Gen Laws § 93A-9-1)
