Operation 3:

Iranian Attackers Breach US Critical Infrastructure

Description:

  • Iranian government-sponsored APT actors had been exploiting vulnerabilities in Microsoft Exchange and Fortinet products to gain initial access to systems across multiple U.S. critical infrastructure sectors, including Transportation, Healthcare and U.S. municipal government. The actors could potentially leverage this access for follow-up operations like data exfiltration, ransomware deployment, and extortion. This has occurred from March 2021 until about November 2021.
  • In March of 2021, the FBI and CISA observed Iranian government-sponsored APT actors scanning devices on ports 4443, 8443, and 10443 for Fortinet FortiOS vulnerability CVE-2018-13379.
  • In May 2021, these Iranian government-sponsored APT actors exploited a Fortigate appliance to access a webserver hosting the domain for a U.S. municipal government.
  • This goal of this campaign was to have initial access to potential exploit a system further in the future.

Offensive Cyber Strategy:

  1. Vulnerability Exploits:
    1. CVEs used:
      1. CVE-2021-34473 (Microsoft Exchange)
      2. CVE-2018-13379, CVE-2020-12812, and CVE-2019-5591(Fortninet).
  2. Mimikatz:
    1. Used for credential theft.:
  3. WinPEAS:
    1. Used for privilege escalation.
  4. SharpWMI (Windows Management Instrumentation):
    1. Used for lateral movement and remote execution.
  5. WinRAR:
    1. Used to archive collected data.
  6. FileZilla:
    1. Used to transfer files.
    2. Potentially used FTP over 443.
  7. Persistence Techniques:
    1. Established new user accounts. Some of these were made to look like other user accounts.
  8. Ransomeware:
    1. BitLocker was forced to activate on host networks, along with a threatening text file or email to the user.
      1. The following emails were left for contact information:
        1. sar_addr@protonmail[.]com
        2. WeAreHere@secmail[.]pro

        4. nosterrmann@protonmail[.]com

Legal Issues:

International Law Considerations:

  • Use of Force: The attacks on water facilities and other critical infrastructure could potentially be considered a use of force under international law. This raises questions about the legality of such actions under the UN Charter.
  • State Responsibility: If conclusively attributed to Iran, these attacks could be considered violations of international law norms prohibiting intervention in the internal affairs of other states and the obligation to respect the sovereignty of other nations.

Laws of Armed Conflict

  • Distinction: Targeting civilian infrastructure like water facilities and hospitals raises concerns about compliance with the principle of distinction between military and civilian targets.
  • Proportionality: The potential impacts on civilian populations from disrupting critical infrastructure could be considered disproportionate to any military objective.

Cybercrime Laws:

  • Domestic and International Cybercrime Statures: The unauthorized access of computer systems, data theft, and deployment of ransomware likely violate numerous domestic and international cybercrime laws.

Human Rights Law:

  • Right to Water and Health: Attacks on water systems and healthcare facilities could be seen as violations of fundamental human rights to water, sanitation, and health.

State Practice and Cyber Norms:

  • Responsible State Behavior: These operations contravene emerging norms of responsible state behavior in cyberspace, particularly those prohibiting attacks on critical infrastructure during peacetime.

Countermeasures and Self-Defense:

  • Proportionate Response: The attacks could potentially justify proportionate countermeasures.

Espionage Considerations:

  • State Practice: While espionage itself is not generally prohibited under international law, the methods used (hacking, data theft) likely violate domestic laws of the target state.

Related Tools, Techniques, Technology, and Data:

  1. Task Scheduler modifications for persistence
  2. Creation of new user accounts for maintaining access.
  3. Use of legitimate tools for data collection and exfiltration
  4. FTP transfers over port 443 for data exfiltration.
  5. Exploiting Fortninet Devices

Defensive Cyber Strategy:

  1. Detection Systems:
    1. Investigate Microsoft Exchange servers for compromise.
    2. Search for potential changes in firewall, RDP, and WinRM settings that may allow attackers back in.
    3. Review directories for new user accounts.
    4. An IDS or IPS can also be implemented
  2. Patch Management:
    1. install new patches and updates.
    2. Specifically patch software affected by the CVE mentioned earlier.
  3. Network Segmentation:
    1. Segmentate the network to prevent lateral movement.
  4. Multi-Factor Authentication:
    1. Use MFA to help protect accounts with access to critical systems.
  5. Phishing Awareness:
      6. Use meetings or emails to spread awareness for phishing attempts.

Legal Issues:

Domestic Legal Authority:

  • Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) Act: This law authorizes CISA to coordinate and implement cybersecurity measures to protect federal civilian networks and critical infrastructure.

Domestic Legal Authority:

  • Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) Act: This law authorizes CISA to coordinate and implement cybersecurity measures to protect federal civilian networks and critical infrastructure.
  • National Defense Authorization Act: Provides legal basis for the Department of Defense to conduct cyber operations to defend against foreign threats to US interests.

International Law Considerations:

  • Sovereignty: Defensive measures must respect the sovereignty of other nations. Actions that impact systems in other countries could violate this principle.
  • Due Diligence: The US has an obligation under international law to exercise due diligence in preventing its territory from being used for cyber operations that affect the rights of other states.

State Responsibility:

  • Attribution:: The US must have sufficient evidence to attribute the attacks to Iran before taking any countermeasures.

Use of Force and Self-Defense:

  • Threshold and Armed Attack: Defensive measures must not cross the threshold of "use of force" under international law unless the original attack reaches the level of an "armed attack," justifying self-defense under Article 51 of the UN Charter.

Human Rights Law:

  • Privacy and Data Protection: Defensive measures involving monitoring and data collection must balance security needs with privacy rights and data protection laws.

Cybercrime Laws:

  • Budapest Convention: US actions should align with international cybercrime treaties, which provide frameworks for international cooperation in cybercrime investigations.

Proportionality and Necessity:

  • Countermeasures: Any defensive actions that could be considered countermeasures must be proportional to the injury suffered and necessary to induce compliance with international law.

Critical Infrastructure Protection

  • Sector-Specific Regulations: Defensive actions in specific sectors must comply with sector-specific regulations and standards.

Public-Private Partnerships:

  • Information Sharing: Legal frameworks like the Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act govern how the government can share threat intelligence with private sector entities.
