IRAN DOCTRINE

Our nation's doctrine constitutes the following:

  1. Asymmetric Warfare
    • Iran views cyberattacks as part of its asymmetric military capabilities to confront more powerful adversaries. This allows Iran to project power despite limitations in its conventional military forces.
  2. Organizational Structure
    • Iran has created a sophisticated organizational structure to manage cyber conflict, including:
      1. The Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC)
      2. The Basij, a civilian paramilitary organization
      3. The Passive Defense Organization (NPDO)
      4. The Supreme Council of Cyberspace for coordination
  3. Defensive and Offensive Capabilities
    • Iran focuses on both protecting its own infrastructure and developing offensive cyber capabilities. The country has a cyber defense system called NIN (National Information Network) to thwart attacks.
  4. Use of Proxies
    • Iran often leverages third parties and proxy groups to carry out cyberattacks, making attribution more difficult. This aligns with Iran's general preference for using ambiguity in achieving its policy goals.
  5. Retaliation and Deterrence
    • Iran sees cyberattacks as part of a continuum of conflict and uses them for retaliation against perceived threats.
  6. Intelligence Gathering
    • Iran conducts cyber espionage operations to collect intelligence on adversaries and dissidents.
  7. Domestic Control
    • Cyberwarfare capabilities are used internally for surveillance and control of Iran's own population.
  8. Proportionality
    • Iranian cyber retaliation tends to be proportionate in scale to the perceived offense.
  9. Integration with Broader Strategy
    • Cyberwarfare is viewed as part of Iran's broader "soft war" military strategy.
