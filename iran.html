Iran'a Doctrine Page:

Iran's cyberwarfare doctrine can be characterized by the following key elements:

  1. Asymmetric warfare: Iran views cyberattacks as part of its asymmetric military capabilities to confront more powerful adversaries. This allows Iran to project power despite limitations in its conventional military forces.
  2. Organizational structure: Iran has created a sophisticated organizational structure to manage cyber conflict, including:
    1. The Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC)
    2. The Basij, a civilian paramilitary organization
    3. The Passive Defense Organization (NPDO)
    4. The Supreme Council of Cyberspace for coordination
  3. Defensive and offensive capabilities: ran focuses on both protecting its own infrastructure and developing offensive cyber capabilities. The country has a cyber defense system called NIN (National Information Network) to thwart attacks.
  4. Use of proxies: Iran often leverages third parties and proxy groups to carry out cyberattacks, making attribution more difficult. This aligns with Iran's general preference for using ambiguity in achieving its policy goals.
  5. Retaliation and deterrence: Iran sees cyberattacks as part of a continuum of conflict and uses them for retaliation against perceived threats.
  6. Intelligence gathering: Iran conducts cyber espionage operations to collect intelligence on adversaries and dissidents.
  7. Domestic control: Cyberwarfare capabilities are used internally for surveillance and control of Iran's own population.
  8. Proportionality: Iranian cyber retaliation tends to be proportionate in scale to the perceived offense.
  9. Integration with broader strategy: Cyberwarfare is viewed as part of Iran's broader "soft war" military strategy.

  1. The Computer Crimes Law of July 1, 2009 or January 2010:
    • Summary: This legislation entitles a series of laws that cover the following crimes: those against data privacy and computer systems, those against data integrity and authenticity, computer-related theft and fraud, and those against public morality.
    • Key Articles
      • Article 1: Illegal access to data, computers and telecommunication systems, any from the government, protected by security measures is criminalized.
      • Article 2: Illegal access to content being transmitted through non-public communica
      • Article 3: Access to and sharing of confidential government information is criminalized. Three degrees of this offense is as follows:
        • Provision (a): Anyone who accesses or obtains confidential data, or spying on confidential content being transmitted is to be prosecuted.
        • Provisions (b) and (c): Individuals who make confidential data available to unauthorized individuals or foreign governments, organizations, companies or groups are to be prosecuted.
      • Article 5: Government officials trained and appointed accountable for the protection of confidential data are criminally liable for Article 3(b) and (c) offenses.
      • Article 14: Producing, sending, publishing, distributing, saving or financially engaging in obscene content by using computer or telecommunication systems or portable data systems is criminalized.
      • Article 16: The use of a computer or telecommunication system to alter or manipulate someone else’s image, audio or video file and publish it in a way that brings disrepute to that person is criminalized.
      • Article 17: The use of a computer or telecommunication system to publish or make available someone else’s personal or family images, audio or video files, or secrets without their consent in a way that brings disrepute to that person is criminalized.
      • Article 18: This carries two distinct components:
        • The use of a computer or telecommunications to “disseminate lies” with the intention of damaging the public, disturbing the public state of mind or disturbing the official authorities’ state of mind is criminalized.
        • The use of a computer or telecommunication system to associate a natural or legal person, or official authorities with a lie, regardless of whether the victim befalls financial or mental damage is criminalized.

  2. The Electronic Commerce Law of the Islamic Republic of Iran (2004):
    • Summary: This legislation addresses e-commerce transactions and data protection. It covers obligations for parties in online contracts, consumer rights when it comes to e-commerce, and protection of personal data.
    • Key Articles:
      • Article 67 (Fraud): Anyone who deceives others or misleads automated systems during an electronic transaction by illegally using data messages, programs, or computer systems, and gains property or financial benefits through actions such as hacking, altering, or interfering with these systems, is considered an offender.
      • Article 68 (Forgery): Anyone who falsifies data messages with financial or legal value, by hacking, altering, or deleting them, or by interfering with computer systems and processing, or using encryption tools (like private keys) without permission to create a signature, or by creating a signature without proper registration, is considered an offender.
      • Article 633-37: This outlines the regulations sellers and service providers must follow in electronic transactions.
        • Article 33: Before entering into a contract, they must provide consumers with key information, including technical specifications, supplier identity, contact details, total expenses, and contract terms.
        • Article 34: After agreeing to the preliminary terms, the supplier must send further details like business address, after-sales service, and contract cancellation terms.
        • Article 35: This information must be delivered clearly and in a durable format.
        • Article 36: For phone transactions, the seller's identity and the purpose of the call must be made clear upfront.
        • Article 37: Consumers have the right to withdraw from distance contracts within at least seven working days without penalty, only covering return costs.
      • Article 51: Suppliers advertising for the sale of their goods and services endangering people's health are to be prosecuted.
      • Article 58: Storing, processing or distributing private "data messages" which may reveal tribal or ethnic origins, moral and religious beliefs, ethical characteristics, and “data messages” regarding the physical, psychological, or sexual condition of people, without their explicit consent is illegal.
      • Article 74 (Violation of Author’s Right): In an electronic transaction, anyone who violates laws protecting the rights of authors, composers, artists, or the laws on translation, copying of books, publications, audio products, and computer software will be prosecuted.
      • Article 75 Using electronic means to obtain or disclose trade secrets for competitive advantage, personal gain, or to harm businesses, and in breach of an employment contract that prohibits revealing such secrets is illegal.
      • Article 66 To protect consumers' rights and promote fair competition in electronic transactions, using trademarks as domain names or in any online manner that misleads or deceives others about the authenticity of goods or services is illegal.
