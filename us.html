Federal Cyber Laws:

  • Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA):
    • Enacted in 1986
    • Amends Comprehensive Crime Control Act of 1984
      • First major cybercrime legislation and was not very effective
    • Prohibits intentionally accessing a computer without authorization or exceeding authorization
    • Has been amended 9 times between 1988 and 2008
    • Criminalizes:
      • Distribution of malicious code
      • Undertaking denial of service attacks
      • Trafficking in passwords or other access control mechanisms
      • Expanding protection to private computers
      • Assing tampering and attempted extortion
      • Including taking information off of systems
      • Including civil actions under the auspices of the act
        • Companies can threaten to use the act against others
        • But can also get judgement easier when evidence isn’t rock solid because proof doesn’t need to be “beyond a reasonable doubt” but rather “a lot” (preponderance)
    • Offenses:
      • Obtaining National Security Information (10 yrs)
      • Accessing a Computer and Obtaining Information (1 – 5 yrs)
      • Trespassing in a Government Computer (1 yr)
      • Accessing a Computer to Defraud and Obtain Value (5 yrs)
      • Intentionally Damaging by Knowing Transmission (1 – 10 yrs)
      • Recklessly Damaging by Intentional Access (1 – 5 yrs)
      • Negligently Causing Damage and Loss by Intentional Access (1 yr)
      • Trafficking in Passwords (1 yr)
      • Extortion Involving Computers (5 yrs)
      • Attempt and Conspiracy to Commit such an Offense (10 yrs)
  • Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act (CISA)
    • Passed in 2015
    • CISA becomes central hub for sharing of “defensive measures” and “cyber threat indicators” between private and public sectors
    • Terms:
      • Cyber threat indicators: necessary info to identify threats and information on actual or potential harm as a result of a cyber threat
      • Defense measures: something that detects, prevents, or mitigates a known or suspected cyber threat or security vulnerability
      • Cybersecurity purpose: purpose of protecting an information system or information form a cyber threat/security vulnerability
    • Encourages private companies to voluntarily share information by allowing federal antitrust exemptions, immunity from federal/state disclosure laws, and a non-waiver of applicable protections for sharing materials
    • Before, companies didn’t share for fear of violating regulations, this act offers protections so companies will share

State-Level Cyber Laws:

  • Security Breach Notification Laws:
    • Summary: Private organizations or Government Institutions operating in the US, are required by law to send out alerts when a data breach occurs, leaking personally identifiable information about individuals.
    • State-Level governments may legislate these practices differently from state to state.
    • Wyoming: Wyo. Stat §§ 40-12
      • Only applies to private organizations. Data theft from Government organizations may be handled differently.
      • Criminalizes:
        • Failure to notify affected individual when security breach occurs
        • Failure to notify the Attorney General if data breach affects more than 1000 residents
        • Failure to provide reasonable security measures when handling sensitive user data.
        • Failure to dispose sensitive legacy, or unauthorized data properly.
        • Providing false information to regulators.
      • Offenses:
        • Fines may vary based on the severity of violation. (Wyo. Stat §§ 40-12-206)
        • Affected individuals may be given the right to pursue legal action against the breached organization.
        • Misdemeanor.
        • Unintentional violation
          • Up to a $750 fine per violation.
          • Possible 6 months imprisonment
        • Intentional Violation:
          • Up to a $10,000 fine per violation
          • Possible 1 year imprisonment
    • Massachusetts: (Mass. Gen Laws § 93H-1 et seq)
      • Applies to both Private and Government Organizations
      • Criminalizes:
        • Failure to notify affected individuals when a data breach occurs.
        • Failure to implement reasonable security measures.
        • Failure to notify Massachusetts Attorney General.
        • Providing false information to regulators.
      • Offenses:
        • Enforcement from the FTC. (Gen Laws § 93H-3)
        • Criminal offence
        • $5000 fine per violation
        • Affected individuals may be granted the right to pursue legal action against the breached organization.
