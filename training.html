Training Program 1: General Offensive and Defense Training

Author: Maya Hampton

Goals:

  • Enhance technical skills and practical experience
  • Keep team informed about latest cyber threats and mitigation strategies
  • Improve incident response coordination and effectiveness

Phase 1: Foundational Knowledge

Objective: Strengthen understanding of foundational cybersecurity principles

Workshops:

  • Understanding cyber threats
  • Advanced networking and security protocols

Knowledge Check:

  • Online quiz on fundamental concepts (OWASP, MITRE ATT&CK, etc)

Phase 2: Hands-On Technical Training

Objective: Develop expertise in tools, threat hunting, and incident management

Tools:

  • Use Wireshark for packet analysis
  • Penetration testing with Metasploit
  • Analyzing malware behavior in a Sandbox
  • Log analysis with Splunk

Labs:

  • Configure firewalls and IDS/IPS in a virtual lab
  • Analyze a simulated phishing attack and trace the attacker

Phase 3: Simulation Exercises

Apply learned skills in simulated real-world scenarios

Capture the Flag:
  • Team members work to find and exploit vulnerabilities in a controlled environment
Incident Response Simulation:
  • Create a containment strategy, communication plan, and a detailed post-mortem report for a simulated ransomware outbreak in the network
Red Team/Blue Team Exercise:
  • Red team conducts a simulated attack and blue team analyzes and defends the attack
  • Teams swap roles the next day

Phase 4: Knowledge Sharing and Feedback

Objective: Foster collaboration and improvement through reviews and discussions

Post-mortem review:

  • Analyze performance during simulation exercises
  • Identify areas for improvement

Team presentations:

  • Each team member presents on a specific recent threat or defense technique

Feedback session:

Training Program 2: Cyber Threat Intelligence Training

Author: Sterling Dawson

Duration: 5-Weeks

Objective:

  • Enhance awareness of evolving cyber threats and tactics.
  • Train staff to effectively use and interpret threat intelligence feeds.
  • Improve incident response capabilities.
  • Build a proactive approach to threat mitigation.

Training Framework Outline

  • Introduction to Threat Intelligence
  • Threat Landscape
  • Hands-on Analysis Workshops
  • Threat Hunting and Response Exercises
  • Simulations

Schedule:

Week 1 - Introduction and Case Studies of Recent Events
  • 2-hour lectures for 7 days
  • Topics covered:
    1. Tools (See tool/feed section)
    2. Platforms/'Feeds' (See tool/feed section)
    3. Well documented current threats
    4. Case studies of recent breaches at other organizations
    5. Recognizing social engineering
    6. Detection of compromise
Week 2-3 - Workshops
  • Daily 1-hour workshops
  • Topics covered:
    1. Practical Threat Hunting with Intelligence Feeds
    2. Malware Analysis and Reverse Engineering
    3. Simulating AI-Driven Phishing Attacks
    4. Incident Response and Forensics Strategies
    5. Exploit Management
    6. Deepfakes
    7. Automation
    8. Red Team/Blue Team Exercise
  • Team discussion for use cases within government organization
Week 4 - Two Day Simulated Attack Scenario
  1. Invite the red team to attack each department for 2 days after training to show possible vulnerabilities in the system.
    • The red team can be in-house or contracted.
  2. The training attack needs to have a strict scope during the first engagement to give departments time to react to each breach without concerns of PII or other sensitive data being breached.
Week 5-Current - Continuous Learning
  1. Weekly updates of ongoing attack vectors
  2. Weekly updates on emerging threat groups
  3. Monthly review of current processes
  4. Quarterly tabletop simulated incident response
  5. Yearly (Unplanned) penetration testing

Resources and Tools Needed for Training:

  1. Threat Research Tools and Platforms:
    • Anomali
    • ThreatConnect
    • MISP (Malware Information Sharing Platform)
    • Cimtrak
  2. Tools for Defense:
    • Cisco Umbrella
    • Zscaler
    • Nessus
    • OpenVAS
    • Splunk
    • ELK Stack
    • CrowdStrike
    • Cortex XSOAR
  3. Offensive Tools:
    • Metasploit
    • Burp
    • Nmap
    • Gophish
    • Cobalt Strike
    • Wireshark
    • John the Ripper

Metrics for Success:

  • Faster detection of potential threats in simulations.
  • Higher accuracy in interpreting threat intelligence.
  • Improved response time during incident handling.
  • Feedback from participants on readiness and skill improvement.

Training Program 3: Social Engineering

Author: Marcus Reinhardt

Timeline:

  • Week 1â€“2: Goals and Foundational Knowledge Sessions.
  • Week 3â€“5: Hands-On Technical Training (split by department).
  • Week 6â€“7: Simulation Exercises.
  • Week 8: Knowledge Sharing and Feedback.

Goals

  • Objective: Established purpose and outcomes of the program.
  • Mission: To build resilience against social engineering by equipping employees and stakeholders with the knowledge, skills, and tools to detect and respond effectively to such threats.
  • Measurable Outcomes:
    • Reduction in phishing click rates by 80%.
    • Increased reporting of suspected social engineering attempts.
    • Enhanced awareness of real-world social engineering tactics.
  • Audience Identification: Target all organizational levelsâ€”IT staff, leadership, and general employees.

Foundational Knowledge

  • Objective: Provide theoretical understanding of social engineering.
  • Core Topics:
    • Overview of Social Engineering: Definition, methods (e.g., phishing, pretexting, baiting).
    • Real-world Examples: Case studies of successful and failed attacks.
    • Psychological Principles: Trust exploitation, authority abuse, urgency tactics.
    • Consequences: Data breaches, financial loss, reputational damage.
  • Delivery Methods:
    • Interactive presentations (in-person or virtual).
    • Handouts and digital guides.
    • Pre-training quizzes to gauge initial understanding.

Hands-On Technical Training

  • Objective: Equip technical staff with skills to identify and mitigate social engineering attempts.
  • IT Staff Training:
    • Configuring email filters to block phishing emails.
    • Monitoring network activity for signs of social engineering attacks.
    • Incident response workflows for suspected attacks.
  • General Staff Training:
    • Identifying phishing emails (e.g., suspicious URLs, fake domains).
    • Safeguarding personal information in professional interactions.
    • Secure communication practices (e.g., encrypting sensitive data).

Simulation Exercises

  • Objective: Test knowledge and practice responses in a controlled environment.
  • Simulated Attacks:
    • Phishing Campaigns: Send realistic phishing emails to employees and analyze their responses.
    • Pretexting Scenarios: Role-play interactions where attackers attempt to elicit sensitive information.
    • Physical Security Tests: Simulate tailgating or unauthorized access attempts to facilities.
  • Evaluation Metrics: Track participation, success rates, and response times.

Knowledge Sharing and Feedback

  • Objective: Encourage ongoing improvement and foster a culture of shared learning.
  • Debriefing Sessions:
    • Analyze results of simulation exercises with participants.
    • Share lessons learned from real-world incidents.
  • Feedback Mechanisms:
    • Conduct post-training surveys.
    • Establish channels for employees to share suspicious activity.
    • Regularly review and refine training materials based on feedback.
  • Recognition Programs:
    • Reward employees who demonstrate exceptional awareness and proactive reporting.
    • Implement the tools that were found to be most effective.

Training Program 4: CTF Training Exercises

Author: Tristan Paulsen

Objectives:

  • Skill Development in regions like cryptography, web security, binary exploitation, reverse engineering, and AI-based threat analysis.
  • Build teamwork and communication skills.
  • Increase adaptability by using a variety of challenges, especially those involving AI or ML systems.
  • Keep up to date by exploring new threats and defensive strategies utilizing AI.

Outline:

  • Week 1-2 (Preparation):
    • Objective: Build foundational skills in CTF topics and AI concepts.
    • Activities:
      • Introductory CTF workshops.
      • Training on AI security concepts.
      • Familiarize team with tools such as Burp Suite, Wireshark, and CyberChef.
  • Week 3-4 (Hands-on Labs and Practice):
    • Objective: Apply skills in hands-on labs.
    • Activities:
      • Practice challenges on CTFs.
      • AI-focused labs.
      • Adversarial AI CTF: Simulate attacks on AI models.
  • Week 5-6 (Strengthening the Team):
    • Objective: Strengthen collaboration and team dynamics.
    • Activities:
      • Rotate team members for different CTFs and assign different roles.
  • Week 7 (Full CTF Competition):
    • Objective: Simulate a real-world competition.
    • Activities:
      • Organize a CTF for team members to compete with one another.
      • Add a wide variety of challenges, including newer vulnerabilities and exploits (AI/ML model exploitation).
      • Use a scoring system to create a sense of competition.
  • Week 8+ (Review and Constant Updates):
    • Objective: Reflect on the training and continue learning.
    • Activities:
      • Go over the competition results with the team.
      • Share strengths and weaknesses.
      • Team members share their thoughts with others.
      • Encourage team members to stay up to date with new CTFs online along with keeping up with cyber news.
      • Repeat on a semi-annual basis.

Resources/Tools Needed:

  • CTF Platforms/CTF Creator.
  • General Tools:
    • Burp Suite
    • CyberChef
    • Wireshark
    • Ghidra (Reverse Engineering)
    • VMs for CTF challenges
  • Learning Materials:
    • Cyber news sources
    • CTF walkthroughs
    • Malware analysis articles/books
    • AI news and learning materials

Training Program 5: In-House Cybersecurity Programs and Exercises

Author: Alonso Vazquez Tena

Comprehensive Training Program:

In-house programs and exercises that will enable the cyber team to stay informed and current include the following:

Technical Skills Development:

A robust technical training program that covers the following crucial cybersecurity topics is recommended to be established:

  • Threat detection and analysis: Weeks 1-2
  • Incident response procedures: Weeks 3-4
  • Vulnerability assessment: Weeks 5-6
  • Secure coding practices: Weeks 7-8
  • Network security: Weeks 9-10

This can be done yearly to ensure the team stays up-to-date on the latest threats and solutions.

Soft Skills and Compliance Training

A training program on soft skills and compliance should be recommended to be undertaken:

  • Communication and teamwork: Week 1
  • Regulatory awareness and compliance: Week 2
  • Ethics in cybersecurity: Week 3
  • Critical thinking and problem-solving: Week 4
Certification Support

Beyond these training programs, it should be encouraged for every team member to obtain industry-recognized certifications like:

  • CompTIA Security+
  • CISSP
  • CEH

Hands-on Exercises

  • Live Fire Exercises:

    Simulated cyber attacks can be conducted to help the team practice defending against real-world threats. These exercises should allow the cyber team to:

    • Improve rapid response capabilities.
    • Work together under pressure.
    • Develop their technical skills realistically.
  • Threat Hunting Exercises:

    Exercises where the team works together to identify and mitigate potential threats should be considered. These exercises help with:

    • Enhancing collaborative skills.
    • Improving decision-making.
    • Developing proactive threat detection capabilities.
  • Capture the Flag (CTF) Competitions:

    Exercises disguised as Capture the Flag competitions can help the team by:

    • Encouraging creativity in problem-solving.
    • Improving vulnerability identification skills.
    • Fostering a competitive and engaging learning environment.
