Operation 1:

Hackers Breach Israeli Nuclear Facility's Computer Network

Description:

  • An Iran-linked hacking group breached the network of an Israeli nuclear installation as a protest against the war in Gaza. They claim to have stolen and published thousands of documents. While many of the documents stolen were not sensitive in nature, the leaked phone numbers, email addresses, and more could be used maliciously in future attacks.
  • Breached the Dimona nuclear facility.
  • Done by Anonymous hackers.
  • Part of a series of data dumps carried out against Israel by various threat groups.

Offensive Cyber Strategy:

In this context, the goal of the Iranian hackers was to infiltrate the IT network, exfiltrate sensitive data, and release it without disrupting the operational technology (OT) network. This type of operation is typically part of an intelligence-gathering and disinformation campaign. While the disruption of the OT was not the goal, it very easily could have been, and could have been disastrous if exploited.

Offensive Cyber Tools/Weapons:

    Spear Phishing Toolkits:

    • Purpose: Used to craft highly targeted phishing emails that exploit specific individuals or groups within the organization to gain network access.
    • Function: By embedding malicious links or attachments, the attackers can execute remote access or data exfiltration once a user is compromised.

    Custom Malware (e.g., Data Exfiltration Malware)

    • Purpose: To extract sensitive data from the compromised network without detection.
    • Function: The malware is designed to remain stealthy, communicate with command and control (C2) servers, and exfiltrate sensitive documents over time.

    Lateral Movement Tools (e.g., Mimikatz)

    • Purpose: To escalate privileges and move laterally within the IT network.
    • Function: After gaining initial access, tools like Mimikatz help attackers gain further control by stealing credentials, which can be used to access sensitive systems and databases.

Legal Issues:

International Law Considerations:
  • Use of Force: The scale and effects of the cyber operation, including the theft of sensitive data and potential impact on critical infrastructure, may reach the threshold of an armed attack.
  • State Responsibility: If the Iranian government is proven to be behind the attack, it could be held responsible for violating international law. However, attribution in cyberspace is difficult, allowing states to maintain plausible deniability.
Domestic Legal Frameworks:
  • Criminal Law: The hackers' actions likely violate Israeli cybercrime laws, including unauthorized access to computer systems and theft of data.
  • National Security Laws: The targeting of a nuclear facility implicates national security concerns. Israeli authorities may invoke special powers to respond to the threat.
Espionage and Intelligence Activities:
  • State Practice: While espionage is generally not prohibited by international law, the methods used (hacking and data theft) may violate domestic laws.
  • Countermeasures: Israel may consider taking proportionate countermeasures in response, which could include retaliatory cyber operations against Iranian targets. However, these must comply with international law principles of necessity and proportionality.
Human Rights and Privacy:
  • Data Protection: The theft and publication of personal data raise privacy concerns. Israel may have obligations under domestic and international law to protect the privacy of affected individuals.
Implications for Nuclear Safety:
  • IAEA Safeguards: The breach of a nuclear facility could potentially impact Israel's ability to meet its nuclear safety and security obligations. This may require reporting to and cooperation with the International Atomic Energy Agency.

Related Offensive Tools, Techniques, Technology, and Data:

  • Tools: Phishing kits, malware creation frameworks, penetration testing tools (e.g., Metasploit for exploit development).
  • Techniques:Social engineering, exploiting unpatched vulnerabilities, data exfiltration methods, credential harvesting, and lateral movement.
  • Technology: Encrypted C2 channels, VPNs for anonymity, and the dark web for selling or distributing the leaked data.
  • Data: Sensitive documents, personal identifiable information (PII) of facility personnel, facility schematics, or other classified data.

Defensive Cyber Strategy:

To protect against similar operations, an Israeli facility would need to develop a cybersecurity strategy that protects its IT network, monitors for threats, and ensures the OT network remains isolated from cyber threats. This strategy would implement the below tools and strategies, as well as other basics such as a zero trust policy, reduced physical access, and basic antivirus.

Defensive Cyber Tools/Weapons:

    Intrusion Detection and Prevention Systems (IDPS):
    • Purpose: To detect and block suspicious activity in real-time.
    • Function: IDPS can identify malicious activity such as abnormal traffic, unauthorized access, or malware communications, and automatically block threats.
    Network Segmentation Tools:
    • Purpose:To isolate sensitive networks like the OT network from potential attack vectors.
    • Function: By segregating IT and OT environments, attackers would find it much harder to move laterally and affect critical systems even if the IT network is compromised.
    Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR):
    • Purpose: To monitor endpoints (workstations, servers) for malicious behavior.
    • Function: EDR continuously scans for indicators of compromise (IoCs), including malware, abnormal process behavior, and privilege escalation attempts.
    Related Defensive Tools, Techniques, Technology, and Data:
    • Tools: Firewalls, VPNs, multi-factor authentication (MFA), Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems.
    • Techniques: Segmentation of IT/OT networks, anomaly detection, patch management, penetration testing.
    • Technology: Use of AI/ML for threat detection, encryption protocols to protect data, forensic tools to analyze breaches.
    • Data: Logs from firewalls, IDPS systems, and SIEM platforms that provide real-time information on potential threats and past attack indicators.

Legal Issues:

    Compliance with National Legislation:
    • Data Protection: The use of monitoring tools like Intrusion Detection and Prevention Systems (IDPS) and Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) must comply with Israel's Protection of Privacy Law (PPL). These systems collect and process data, potentially including personal information, which requires adherence to data protection principles.
    • Cybersecurity Obligations: The new Temporary Law on Handling Severe Cyberattacks imposes specific obligations on digital service providers. This includes reporting severe cyberattacks to authorized agencies and following their instructions for cyber defense.
    Government Oversight and Intervention:
    • Mandatory Reporting: Under the new law, the facility may be required to report cybersecurity incidents to government agencies.
    • Government Access: Authorized agencies may request access to the facility's systems and data for cybersecurity purposes. This must be balanced against privacy rights and conducted within legal boundaries.
    International Law Considerations:
    • Use of Force: While these defensive measures are unlikely to constitute a use of force under international law, any active countermeasures must be carefully considered to avoid escalation.
    • State Responsibility: Israel must ensure that its cybersecurity practices, even when implemented by private entities, do not violate international law.
    Sector-Specific Regulations:
    • Critical Infrastructure: If the facility is considered critical infrastructure, it may be subject to additional regulations under the Regulation of Security in Public Bodies Law, which authorizes specific agencies to issue binding directives.
    Privacy and Civil Liberties:
    • Employee Privacy: The use of EDR and other monitoring tools must be balanced against employee privacy rights. Clear policies and consent procedures may be necessary.
    • Data Minimization: Tools collecting data must adhere to the principle of data minimization, collecting only what is necessary for cybersecurity purposes.
    Liability and Due Diligence:
    • Standard of Care: Implementing these defensive measures may be considered part of the standard of care for cybersecurity. Failure to do so could potentially lead to liability issues in case of a breach.

Operation 2:

Israeli Water Infrastructure Attack

Description:

  • Occurred in April of 2020.
  • Linked to Iranian group: CyberAv3ngers
  • In two rural districts, Iranian hackers attacked computers that control water flow and wastewater treatments.
  • Attack attempted to poison the water by increasing chlorine levels in water going to residential areas.
  • Also sought to trigger fail-safe which would shut down pumps to leave thousands without water during heatwave.
  • Pumps went into continuous operation, wiped out information, irregularities due to unplanned changes in data.
  • Ultimately no damage was done (minor issues in local water systems).
  • Israel retaliated by launching a cyberattack on an Iranian port, attempting to harm the economy.
  • Was first modern attack on civilian infrastructure from Iran to Israel.

Offensive Cyber Strategy:

  • Logical:
    • Exploited Passwords:
      • Many facilities use default passwords so it was easy for hackers to gain access.
    • Exploited known vulnerabilities:
      • Hackers are able to manipulate systems remotely by gaining access through devices with unpatched vulnerabilities.
      • Scanned for devices to find these vulnerabilities.
  • Physical:
    • Manipulated programmable logic controllers to increase chlorine levels and prevent water from being distributed.
    • Program logic controllers are physical devices in the facility that are essential to automate processes in water treatment and distribution systems.
    • Able to physically attack remotely.
  • Summary of related tools:
    • Spear-phishing:
      • Attempt to trick specific individuals into providing sensitive information or downloading malware typically through emails that appear legitimate
    • Lateral Movement:
      • Strategy used by hackers to navigate from one compromised system to gain access to another, more valuable target.
    • Exploiting zero-day vulnerabilities:
      • Exploiting vulnerabilities that the developer is unaware of to gain access to a system before a patch is released
    • Use of encrypted communication channels to obfuscate command-and-control operations:
      • Conceal communication between malware on a compromised system and the attacker’s command server

Legal Concerns:

    International Law Considerations:
    • Use of Force: Attempting to poison water supplies and disrupt critical infrastructure could potentially be considered a use of force under international law.
    • State Responsibility: If conclusively attributed to Iran, the attack could be considered a violation of international law. However, the challenges of attribution in cyberspace often allow states to maintain plausible deniability.
    Domestic and International Criminal Law:
    • Cybercrime: The hackers' actions violate Israeli cybercrime laws, including unauthorized access to computer systems and attempted sabotage of critical infrastructure.
    • Terrorism: Given the potential for mass civilian casualties, the operation could potentially be classified as cyber terrorism under some legal frameworks.
    Laws of Armed Conflict:
    • Distinction: Targeting civilian water supplies raises concerns about the principle of distinction between military and civilian targets in armed conflict.
    • Proportionality: The potential harm to civilians from water poisoning could be considered disproportionate to any military objective.
    Human Rights Law:
    • Right to Water: Attempting to disrupt water supplies could be seen as a violation of the human right to clean water and sanitation.
    State Practice and Cyber Norms:
    • Responsible State Behavior: The operation contravenes emerging norms of responsible state behavior in cyberspace, including refraining from attacking critical infrastructure in peacetime.
    Countermeasures and Self-Defense:
    • Proportionate Response: Israel's retaliatory cyberattack on an Iranian port raises questions about the legality and proportionality of countermeasures in cyberspace.
    Implications for International Security:
    • Escalation Risks: Such operations against critical civilian infrastructure could lead to dangerous escalation between states.

Defensive Cyber Strategy:

  • Logical:
    • Intrusion Detection and Prevention Systems:
      • Monitor network traffic in real-time to detect suspicious behavior or any unauthorized access attempts.
      • This should be implemented as a high priority for any systems directly controlling critical infrastructure.
      • Patterns of malware can be detected and blocked before they reach operational systems.
    • Multi-Factor Authentication:
      • Secure access to critical infrastructure control systems and administrative tools to ensure that access cannot be easily gained.
      • Additional verification, such as a onetime passcode or biometrics, is required to gain access instead of using just a username and password.
      • Credentials can still be stolen, but it is harder to gain access due to the second method of authorization required.
  • Physical:
    • Air-gapped systems:
      • Critical infrastructure control systems isolate from the internet and other networks to prevent remote access.
      • Iran was able to gain control through remote access – this could prevent similar future attacks.
      • A physical attack (such as a USB drop) would be the only way to attack this type of system.
      • Protects against malware, ransomware, zero-day exploits, advanced persistent threats.
      • Access controls and authentication must be strict and data is transferred manually.
  • Summary of Related Tools:
    • Firewalls
      • Barrier between trusted internal networks and untrusted external networks
      • Blocks unauthorized access while allowing legitimate traffics.
    • Segmentation of network zones
      • Divides network into smaller, isolated zones to limit breach impacts
      • If one segment gets compromised, hackers can’t easily move laterally to another.
    • PLC-specific security appliances:
      • Added security features tailored specifically for the programmable logic controllers that are used by the Israeli facilities.
    • Continuous monitoring and logging of network traffic, user access logs, and system performance data.
      • Automated monitoring tools can be used to watch for suspicious behavior to allow quick response to threats

Legal Concerns:

    International Law Considerations
    • Right to Self-Defense: Under international law, Israel's defensive measures must align with the right to self-defense as outlined in Article 51 of the UN Charter.
    • State Responsibility: Israel must ensure that its defensive actions do not infringe on the sovereignty of other states. Attribution of the attack to a state actor like Iran requires clear evidence to justify any retaliatory measures.
    Domestic Legal Frameworks:
    • Data Protection and Privacy: Implementing tools such as Intrusion Detection and Prevention Systems (IDPS) and Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) involves processing potentially sensitive data. These measures must comply with Israeli data protection laws.
    • Critical Infrastructure Protection: Israeli laws governing critical infrastructure security mandate robust defenses against cyber threats. The use of air-gapped systems for critical infrastructure aligns with these requirements by preventing unauthorized remote access.
    Human Rights Considerations:
    • Access to Water: The attack's potential impact on water supply raises human rights concerns regarding access to clean water. Defensive measures must ensure that such essential services are protected from disruption.
    Industry Standards and Best Practices:
    • Security Standards Compliance: Implementing cybersecurity tools must adhere to industry standards and best practices, ensuring that systems are adequately protected against known vulnerabilities and threats.

Operation 3:

Iranian Attackers Breach US Critical Infrastructure

Description:

  • Iranian government-sponsored APT actors had been exploiting vulnerabilities in Microsoft Exchange and Fortinet products to gain initial access to systems across multiple U.S. critical infrastructure sectors, including Transportation, Healthcare and U.S. municipal government. The actors could potentially leverage this access for follow-up operations like data exfiltration, ransomware deployment, and extortion. This has occurred from March 2021 until about November 2021.
  • In March of 2021, the FBI and CISA observed Iranian government-sponsored APT actors scanning devices on ports 4443, 8443, and 10443 for Fortinet FortiOS vulnerability CVE-2018-13379.
  • In May 2021, these Iranian government-sponsored APT actors exploited a Fortigate appliance to access a webserver hosting the domain for a U.S. municipal government.
  • This goal of this campaign was to have initial access to potential exploit a system further in the future.

Offensive Cyber Strategy:

    1. Used for credential theft.:
  3. WinPEAS:
    1. Used for privilege escalation.

    1. Used for lateral movement and remote execution.

Operation 4 && Operation 4.5:

ESET Research: Iran-OilRig group (Helix Kitten) deployed new malware to its Israeli victims, collecting credentials


Actor(s): Oilrig (COBALT GYPSY, IRN2, APT34, Helix Kitten, Evasive Serpens, Hazel Sandstorm, EUROPIUM)

What did they do? Held two campaigns with a undiscovered backdoors.

Who did the attack: Various Israeli healthcare sectors, a manufacturing company, and a local governmental organization.

Type of Attack: Cyberespionage and credential theft.

1st Campain Caught - September 2023: Credential Theft

2nd campaign Caught - December 2023: Actions on systems (downloaders)

Description:

Oilrig began the campaign (Solar) by hacking into a legitimate webserver to use it as a C2 (c&c) server to run undiscovered backdoors after being compromised typically through email phishing. The c2 server allows Iranian hackers to access the systems without looking like suspicious network activity. Doing this allowed them to also install a multitude of different post-compromise tools that allow for extraction of data. During the first campaign Oilrig mostly focused on stealing data from Windows Credential Manager and from major browsers, credentials, cookies and browsing history.

After Compromise they began a second operation closely related to Solar where they used four lightweight but highly adaptable downloaders; SampleCheck5000, ODAgent, OILcheck, and OILBooster. Although relation of the two compromises hasn't directly been linked, we can assume that the same actors attacked both as all data breaches during the second operation were all previously compromised by Oilrig. Like they did previously during the Solar campain, they used malicious emails, but this time using legitimate cloud storage and cloud-based email services most of which come from Microsoft. Notable compromises included; Microsoft Graph OneDrive, Outlook APIs’, and Microsoft Office Exchange Web Services.

Tools Used:

  • ODagent: (similar to OilRig’s Marlin backdoor) Uses the Microsoft OneDrive API for C&C communications. However, ODagent was specifically made to specialize in downloading and executing various payloads.
  • OILboost: like ODAgent, it uses Microsoft one drive, but the difference is in they use the API to access OneDrive accounts instead of a compromised Outlook account.
  • Samplecheck5000 (SC5k): Is used to download and execute OilRig tools using the Office Exchange Web Services instead of OneDrive. However, it was updated to now obsfiscate analysis and add new new exfiltration functionality. This downloader (Last seen 2023) has been updated to v3.
  • OILcheck: Based on SC5k v3. Uses the same methods to extract commands hidden in messeges, but the difference being is uses Microsoft graph for communication instead of Microsoft exchange mail

Offensive Cyber Strategy:

    Custom Phishing Campaign Engine with Dynamic Payload Generation:
    • Tool: Gophish or Cobalt Strike
    • Purpose: Automate the crafting and distribution of phishing emails with embedded malicious payloads that can adapt based on the target’s system. Cobalt Strike, in particular, is popular among adversaries for its flexibility in creating payloads and managing phishing campaigns.
    Stealth C2 Infrastructure with Domain Fronting Capabilities:
    • Tool: Merlin C2 Platform or Covenant C2 Framework
    • Purpose: Set up a command-and-control (C2) server using domain fronting to blend malicious traffic with legitimate communications. These C2 tools can disguise traffic through trusted domains, making it difficult for defenders to distinguish between normal and malicious activities.
    Modular Data Exfiltration Malware with Adaptive Capabilities:
    • Tool: Gophish or Cobalt Strike
    • Purpose: Use modular malware to deploy adaptive backdoors that can extract credentials, cookies, and browsing history based on conditions. Empire’s PowerShell scripts can stay dormant and trigger based on specific network conditions.
